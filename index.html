Sued for Debt?

Your Step-by-Step Guide to Responding

Don't Ignore It

The worst thing you can do is nothing. If you do not respond, the court enters a default judgment -- giving the creditor the right to garnish your wages, levy your bank account, and lien your property. You have rights, but only if you show up.

What happens with a default judgment.

Your Response Options

File an answer and raise defenses (statute of limitations, wrong amount, lack of standing). Negotiate a settlement (typically 40-60% of the balance). File bankruptcy to stop the lawsuit and eliminate the debt. Claim exemptions if you are judgment proof.

Detailed defense guide | Are you judgment proof?

Timeline and Deadlines

You typically have 20-30 days to respond (check your summons). If you need more time, you can often request an extension from the court. The plaintiff must prove their case -- you do not have to prove you don't owe the money.

Getting Help

Legal aid organizations in your area. Pro bono attorneys through your state bar. Many consumer protection attorneys work on contingency for FDCPA violations. You can also represent yourself -- many courts have self-help resources.

Filing bankruptcy without a lawyer.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I owe the money?

You still have options. The amount may be wrong, the statute may have expired, or bankruptcy may be the best path. Always file an answer.

Can I settle after being sued?

Yes. Most debt lawsuits settle before trial, often for significantly less than the full amount.

How much does it cost to file an answer?

Usually free or minimal ($20-50 filing fee). Many courts provide fill-in-the-blank answer forms.
